        I've been running a services company for 8 years.

        Yesterday,         Sequoia Capital published a piece arguing that the next $1T company will be a software company disguised as a services firm.

        Most founders read that and got excited about a new SaaS angle. I read it and thought, they just described the business model I've been operating for the last decade.

        Sequoia's thesis is sharp: stop selling the tool, sell the work. Every time Claude or GPT gets smarter, your SaaS moat shrinks. But if you sell the outcome - the books closed, the leads booked, the contract drafted, every model upgrade makes you faster, cheaper, and harder to kill. For every $1 spent on software, $6 is spent on services. The entire tech industry has been fighting over the $1 while the $6 sat there, completely uncontested.

        This is the greatest arbitrage in the history of technology. And almost nobody in tech sees it bc they have a massive blind spot called 𝘁𝗿𝘂𝘀𝘁.

        Here's what Sequoia's piece doesn't say, because it's written by investors, not operators. The hardest part is NOT building the AI. The hardest part is earning the right to replace the human. When we started embedding AI into Belkins Inc. workflows, the tech worked on day one. Clients didn't trust it until month six. Just because a CFO doesn't bet their career on a demo, they bet it on a decade of receipts.

        Software companies trying to "become services firms" are going to smash face-first into this wall. Best AI on earth, zero credibility to deliver an outcome someone's job depends on. Meanwhile, services companies going AI-native already have the trust, the relationships, the contracts, and the proof. They just need the tech and the tech is basically free now.

        I'm not guessing here. I'm watching this happen across our portfolio in real time. The next trillion-dollar company isn't being built in a WeWork in San Francisco by someone pitching "AI-native services." It's a boring accounting firm in Ohio. A staffing agency in London. A lead gen company that's been delivering outcomes for a decade and just quietly replaced its entire cost structure with AI while competitors were still debating which LLM to use.

        The incumbents are about to become the disruptors. And almost nobody sees it coming.

        If you're operating in services right now, here's the playbook:

        → Intelligence work (rules, patterns, repetition) - automate yesterday
        → Judgment work (taste, experience, context) - augment with AI, keep humans
        → Start with outsourced work - budget exists, buyer wants outcomes, swap is painless
        → Expand into insourced work as your AI compounds data nobody else can touch

        This window won't stay open.

        In 18 months, everyone will know this playbook, and the land grab will be over. If you run a services company and you're not rebuilding your delivery layer with AI right now, you're not going to be disrupted by some startup →

        You're going to be disrupted by your competitor who reads this post.

        Most agencies get stuck between $1M and $5M.

        I know, because I’ve acquired agencies in that exact range.

        Here’s what usually happens:
        - They spend $10K to make $10K. No margin.
        - They underinvest in sales + marketing.
        - They burn out delivering for clients while churn kills growth.
        - They refuse to evolve from a “family shop” into a real company with ops, HR, and structure.

        That’s why they plateau.

        Breaking through takes a different mindset:
        ☑️ Double down on sales + marketing, even when it feels uncomfortable.
        ☑️ Invest in ops and client delivery systems that don’t depend on you.
        ☑️ Build for scale, not survival.

        It’s hard – but that’s how we built         Belkins into a $100M agency trusted by Amazon, TikTok, and Cloudflare.
